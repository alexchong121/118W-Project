The Amazon Rainforest and why it's so important

The Amazon Rainforest is the world's biggest rainforest and makes up about
60% of the remaining rainforests on Earth. The greater part of the forest is
located within Brazil. Given the huge quantities of plants and creatures that
live there, the Amazon rainforest is of endless natural and environmental worth.


Planting over 30,000 trees to save the Amazon Rainforest

For quite a long time the individuals in the Manu district of Peru have utilized slash-and-burn
as their main farming technique. Understanding that soon enough there would be no woodland
left if this technique continued, Reynaldo Ochoa started trying different things with various ways to
deal with cultivating the land. He's presently committed to reforesting the locale and showing occupants
increasingly reasonable options in contrast to deforestation, such as agroforestry and permaculture.


Amazon tribes fighting to save their rainforest

Indigenous individuals in Brazil have pledged to secure their territory as vast regions of
the Amazon Rainforest continues to burn. The biggest rainforest on the planet retains
billions of tonnes of CO2 yearly, easing back the pace of global warming. It is additionally
home to about 3 million types of plants and creatures as well as around a million people.


The Waorani tribe in Ecuador is fighting to protect the Amazon Rainforest

Armed with blowdarts and spears, the Waorani tribe state they are prepared to strike down trespassers of their
Amazonian native land, much the same as their ancestors did. In any case, presently their fight is in court, and
their adversaries—Ecuador's administration and oil multinationals—are unremarkable. They're trying to battle for
control over their side of Ecuador's Amazon rainforest, tribal grounds where exploration licenses are currently
available for sale by the government as they intend to offer worthwhile land concessions to oil organizations.
